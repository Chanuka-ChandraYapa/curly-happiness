The question of when a director crosses the line from poor business judgment into actionable breach of duty has never been easy to answer with precision. A trio of decisions handed down by the Supreme Court between 2022 and 2024 has brought us somewhat closer.

## The business judgment rule under pressure

Sri Lankan courts have historically adopted an approach borrowed from English company law: directors are not liable for mere errors of commercial judgment, only for decisions made in bad faith or with gross negligence. The Companies Act No. 7 of 2007 codified this to some degree, but left considerable ambiguity about what "reasonable care" requires in practice.

The 2023 decision in *Mercantile Holdings v. Perera & Others* was notable for what the Court chose not to say. Rather than reaffirm the broad protection of the business judgment rule, the Court confined its analysis to the specific facts — a director who had approved a related-party loan without obtaining any independent valuation — and found liability on grounds of procedural failure rather than outcome.

> The obligation is not merely to make a sound decision, but to ensure that the process by which that decision is made is one that a reasonable director, properly informed, would have followed.

This framing matters. It shifts scrutiny from the decision itself to the deliberative process that preceded it.

## Practical implications for boards

The clearest takeaway from recent case law is procedural: boards that document their deliberations carefully, obtain independent professional advice on material transactions, and recuse conflicted directors from relevant votes are in a substantially stronger position.

Three steps have become near-essential:

- **Independent board committees** for transactions involving related parties or significant conflict of interest
- **Written records** of the information presented to directors before any major approval
- **Proactive disclosure** to shareholders where the Companies Act requires it, and sometimes where it does not

None of this is new in principle. What is new is the willingness of courts to look behind the board resolution and examine the quality of the information on which it was based.

## What remains uncertain

The cases do not yet settle the question of how liability is apportioned when a board acts collectively on advice that later proves wrong. Nor do they address the position of nominee directors — a significant gap given the prevalence of nominee arrangements in Sri Lankan holding structures.

These are questions the courts will inevitably have to address. For now, the prudent approach is to treat the recent decisions as a signal that the standard of care is moving, and to ensure that governance practices are keeping pace with that movement.
